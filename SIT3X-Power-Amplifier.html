SIT3X Power Amplifier
by Lynn H. Quam
The SIT3X is a source-follower (aka. common-drain) audio power amplifier whose output stage (OS) consists of a Tokin 2SK182ES SIT and an IXYS IXTN40P50P PFET.  The SIT3X output stage (OS) design was inspired by the Nelson Pass FirstWattt SIT-3  amplifier and a variety of other designs by Nelson Pass and is discussed in the diyAudio Pass-Labs forum thread The SIT3X Amplifier.   Front end design was inspired by John Curl's Blowtorch Preamplifier.   The resulting SIT3X prototype amplifier has the following properties:
  • No global feedback around either the entire amplifier or within front end stage.
  • Low distortion  
    • < .03% THD at 1W
    • < 1% THD at 50 Watts  8 Ohm
    • < 1% THD at 100 Watts  4 Ohm
  • Voltage gain 20dB
  • Output inverted relative to input
  • Output impedance approximately 0.15R => damping factor of 50
  • Power consumption 360 Watts
  • Weight approximately 50 pounds
  • Dimensions 15"W x 11.5"H x 15"D

Design History:

Output Stage:

The SIT3X evolved from a discovery made during LTSpice simulations of variants of the FirstWatt SIT-3 topology.  Simulations of the SIT-3 topology showed a weird anti push-pull behavior of the circuit which was strongly dependent on SIT/PFET device parameters and source resistors.  Since I had a very limited selection of six 2SK182ES SIT devices available to select from, I was seeking a way control the DEFiSIT parameters with devices that could not work in the FirstWatt SIT-3 circuit.  Another goal was to eliminate the source resistors altogether and obtain higher output power than that of the SIT-3.

The magic A attenuation factor:

While simulating different output stage variations, I discovered a topology which attenuates the AC signal to the PFET gate relative to that of the SIT gate, and independently controls the Vgs bias voltages of the FETs.  This circuit allows the SIT operating point Vds and Id to be chosen freely and different attenuation factors A produce significant changes to the circuit behavior.  In retrospect, I should have named the attenuation factor X for the X in SIT3X. 
A > A_J1ccs push/push (anti push/pull) behavior
A = A_J1ccs  SIT J1 acts as constant current source at low to medium power levels
A = A_ACbal  J1 and M1 have balanced AC push/pull currents
A = A_H2min  2nd harmonic minimized at 1W
A = A_M1ccs  PFET M1 acts as constant current source at low to medium power levels
A < A_M1ccs  pull/pull (anti push/pull) behavior

The values for A_J1ccs, A_ACbal, A_H2min, and A_M1ccs depend on the device and circuit parameters, but in bench testing of the prototype SIT3X right channel I measured:

A_J1ccs = 0.97959
A_ACbal = did not measure
A_H2min = 0.94851
A_M1ccs = 0.92828

Second Harmonic Relative Phase:

Simulations (and subsequent bench tests) of the SIT3X output stage also showed at second harmonic (H2) relative phase (as defined by Nelson Pass) behaved was negative for A>A_H2min and positive for A < A_H2min. 

If we wish to have negative H2 phase and operate in the A > A_H2min we can use a (non-inverting) front end that is neutral with respect to H2 phase and connect the speaker terminals "normally". 

If we wish to have negative H2 phase and operate in the A < A_H2min range of values we can use a front end that inverts the signal and connect the speaker wires inverted. 

Simulations (and subsequent bench tests) showed better harmonic distortion behavior in the A < A_H2min.  

Load on the Front End:

The resistor divider (potentiometer in the circuit shown above) places a resistive load on the input (FEOut).   Bench measurements show that the 2SK182ES has an input capacitance of approximately 19nF.   All of the resistive and capacitive loads combine to present a significant load on the front end circuit.  Using simulations I picked a value of between 500R and 1K for the resistor divider (potentiometer in the schematic).

Front End Stage Circuit Alternatives:

For the SIT3X prototype I chose to use an inverting front end and operate in the A < A_H2min range of values. 

Buffer/Step-Up Transformer:

The FirstWatt SIT-3 amplifier uses a JFET buffer to drive a step-up transformer to provide current gain and voltage gain to drive its SIT/PFET output stage.    I abandoned this approach because of the limited output voltage range of quality audio transformers.  Multiple stacked transformers would be required for a front end circuit capable of more than about 20V peak output, and the transformers introduce significant distortion.
